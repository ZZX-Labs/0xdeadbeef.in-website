Purpose

Writing preserves the reasoning behind the work.

A working system records what was built. Writing records why it was built, how the problem was understood, which alternatives were rejected, where uncertainty remains, and what changed during development.

Technical documentation, essays, project journals, and whitepapers provide the context that source code and finished artifacts cannot preserve by themselves.

The writing archive is therefore part of the engineering record rather than a separate publication activity.

Articles may document confirmed findings, provisional research, open questions, failed approaches, historical analysis, technical methods, policy implications, or personal observations. Each form should identify its own evidentiary status.

Editorial Standards

Standards for publication

Distinguish evidence from interpretation

Observed facts, cited sources, derived values, model output, assumptions, inference, and opinion should remain clearly distinguishable.

Cite primary sources where possible

Original documents, source code, datasets, court records, technical specifications, transcripts, measurements, and direct records should be preferred over repeated secondary claims.

Preserve uncertainty

Do not manufacture confidence where the available evidence remains incomplete, ambiguous, contested, or provisional.

Correct errors visibly

Material corrections should be dated and recorded rather than silently altering the publication history.

Preserve authorship and attribution

Quotations, images, datasets, code, fonts, diagrams, prior research, and third-party materials should retain appropriate credit and licensing information.

Publish limitations

Scope, missing data, unresolved questions, methodological constraints, conflicts of interest, and known weaknesses should be stated directly.

Publication Process

From research note to public document

Stage 01

Collection

Gather source material, field notes, code, measurements, references, images, records, and project evidence.

Stage 02

Drafting

Define the question, audience, scope, argument, structure, methods, and supporting evidence.

Stage 03

Verification

Check factual claims, citations, code examples, figures, data, dates, names, licenses, and technical reproducibility.

Stage 04

Review

Review clarity, security implications, legal risk, privacy, responsible disclosure, and potential downstream harm.

Stage 05

Publication

Publish the document with date, version, authorship, license, citations, metadata, and stable URL.

Stage 06

Revision

Record corrections, updates, changed conclusions, new evidence, retired methods, and later editions.

Collaboration

Editorial and research collaboration

Proposals may include co-authored technical papers, peer review, source verification, historical research, translation, data analysis, visual documentation, publication design, or project-specific editorial work.

Submissions should identify the proposed subject, intended audience, source material, authorship, publication rights, timeline, and any confidentiality or security requirements.

Sensitive material should not be transmitted through ordinary contact channels without prior coordination. Responsible disclosure, protected sources, personal data, unpublished investigations, and controlled technical information may require secure handling.

Publication does not imply automatic endorsement of every contributor's broader views or affiliations.
