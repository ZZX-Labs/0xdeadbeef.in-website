Purpose

Writing preserves the reasoning behind the work.

A working system records what was built. Writing records why it was built, how the problem was understood, which alternatives were rejected, where uncertainty remains, and what changed during development.

Technical documentation, essays, project journals, research reports, and whitepapers preserve context that source code and finished artifacts cannot preserve by themselves.

The writing archive is therefore part of the engineering record rather than a separate publication activity.

Publications may document confirmed findings, provisional research, open questions, failed approaches, historical analysis, technical methods, policy implications, field observations, or personal interpretation. Each work should identify its own evidentiary status.

Plaintext-first publication

Blog entries use a plaintext-first structure built from post.txt, metadata.json, and the shared publication renderer. Additional formats are linked only when the files actually exist.

Editorial Standards

Standards for publication.

Published work should preserve evidence, uncertainty, authorship, limitations, revision history, and the distinction between fact and interpretation.

Distinguish evidence from interpretation

Observed facts, cited sources, derived values, model output, assumptions, inference, speculation, and opinion should remain clearly distinguishable.

Cite primary sources where possible

Original documents, source code, datasets, court records, technical specifications, transcripts, measurements, direct records, and first-party materials should be preferred over repeated secondary claims.

Preserve uncertainty

Do not manufacture confidence where available evidence remains incomplete, ambiguous, disputed, contradictory, provisional, or methodologically limited.

Correct errors visibly

Material corrections should be dated and recorded rather than silently altering publication history. Significant revisions should preserve prior versions where practical.

Preserve authorship and attribution

Quotations, images, datasets, code, fonts, diagrams, prior research, performances, recordings, and third-party materials should retain appropriate credit and licensing information.

Publish limitations

Scope, missing data, unresolved questions, methodological constraints, conflicts of interest, sample limitations, tool limitations, and known weaknesses should be stated directly.

Separate research from advocacy

Where a publication argues for a policy, strategy, design, or political position, the advocacy should remain distinct from the evidentiary record.

Document reproducibility

Technical publications should identify software versions, dependencies, configuration, hardware, inputs, outputs, datasets, build methods, and checksums where practical.

Respect confidentiality and privacy

Protected sources, private records, sensitive personal information, client material, responsible disclosures, and controlled technical information require explicit handling rules.

Review potential harm

Before publication, review whether operational details, personal data, exploit-enabling material, surveillance capability, or infrastructure information could create disproportionate risk.

Use precise language

Avoid inflated claims, unnecessary jargon, unsupported certainty, vague attribution, false universality, and language that obscures who did what, when, and under which conditions.

Preserve the archival record

Final publications should retain stable URLs, publication dates, version identifiers, authorship, licensing, references, and enough context for later readers to understand the work.

Publication Process

From research note to public document.

Publication is treated as a controlled engineering process rather than an informal final step.

Stage 01

Collection

Gather source material, field notes, code, measurements, references, images, records, correspondence, project evidence, and prior research.

Stage 02

Scoping

Define the research question, intended audience, scope, exclusions, assumptions, evidentiary standard, and publication objective.

Stage 03

Drafting

Develop the argument, structure, methods, technical explanation, supporting evidence, limitations, figures, references, and appendices.

Stage 04

Verification

Check factual claims, quotations, citations, code examples, figures, datasets, names, dates, licenses, calculations, and technical reproducibility.

Stage 05

Security and Privacy Review

Review sensitive details, personal data, infrastructure information, disclosure obligations, source protection, downstream harm, and operational risk.

Stage 06

Editorial Review

Review clarity, structure, precision, accessibility, duplication, unsupported claims, citation quality, typography, and consistency.

Stage 07

Publication

Publish the document with date, version, authorship, license, references, metadata, stable URL, downloadable formats, and verification material where available.

Stage 08

Revision

Record corrections, updates, changed conclusions, new evidence, retired methods, superseded recommendations, and later editions.

Stage 09

Archival Preservation

Preserve source files, earlier versions, citations, images, datasets, signatures, checksums, publication manifests, and offline copies.

Verification

Publication integrity and authenticity.

Selected documents may include cryptographic verification, reproducibility records, and archival metadata.

Checksums

SHA-256, SHA3-256, or other published digests may be supplied for downloadable files, source archives, datasets, and document editions.

OpenPGP Signatures

Detached signatures may be published for important reports, formal papers, manifests, and release archives using a verified public key.

Bitcoin Timestamping

Selected publication hashes may be anchored or timestamped using Bitcoin-based systems where durable proof of existence is useful.

Version Records

Material revisions should include a version identifier, revision date, change summary, and relationship to earlier editions.

Source Archives

Source Markdown, plain text, diagrams, data files, code, references, and build instructions may accompany final documents.

Reproducibility Notes

Technical publications should state the environment, dependencies, inputs, commands, expected outputs, and known sources of variation.

Verification status

No article should claim to be signed, hashed, timestamped, peer reviewed, independently reproduced, or externally verified unless the corresponding evidence is actually published.
