Purpose

Research should create understanding, capability, and durable evidence.

Research is treated as a practical process of observation, collection, comparison, modeling, experimentation, implementation, verification, and publication.

The objective is not merely to produce conclusions. It is to preserve enough evidence, source material, methodology, code, configuration, and context that another person can examine how those conclusions were reached.

Projects may produce software, hardware, firmware, datasets, maps, visualizations, technical papers, laboratory records, photographic documentation, prototypes, or operational systems.

Where public release is lawful and responsible, research should be published in a form that others can inspect, reproduce, challenge, extend, or preserve.

Methods

Research methods

Different disciplines require different methods, but all work should preserve provenance, assumptions, uncertainty, and enough context for later review.

Define the question

State the problem, scope, operational context, intended use, exclusions, assumptions, and criteria by which the work will be evaluated.

Collect primary material

Preserve original documents, measurements, code, logs, images, recordings, datasets, artifacts, interviews, and field notes wherever lawful and appropriate.

Record provenance

Track source, author, date, collection method, license, checksum, version, location, and chain of custody where relevant.

Separate fact from interpretation

Distinguish observed evidence, derived values, model output, assumptions, inference, opinion, and unresolved uncertainty.

Build reproducible workflows

Preserve scripts, configuration, versions, dependencies, hardware details, input data, processing steps, and output checksums.

Test competing explanations

Avoid selecting only the evidence that supports an initial conclusion. Compare alternative hypotheses and identify what evidence would disprove each one.

Publish limitations

State where data is incomplete, tools are experimental, samples are biased, measurements are uncertain, or conclusions remain provisional.

Archive the process

Preserve not only the final report, but also the methods, revisions, failed attempts, negative results, source material, and decisions that shaped the work.

Standards

Minimum standards for published research

Every published research project should identify

  • The research question or technical problem
  • Scope and exclusions
  • Methods and tools
  • Data sources and provenance
  • Assumptions and dependencies
  • Security and ethical considerations
  • Known limitations
  • Licensing and attribution

Every reproducible technical project should include

  • Source code or implementation files
  • Installation and build instructions
  • Version and dependency information
  • Example input and output
  • Testing procedure
  • Checksums or signatures where practical
  • Change history
  • Contact or disclosure channel

Ethics and Responsibility

Research capability requires explicit boundaries.

Research involving cybersecurity, surveillance, investigations, sensitive personal data, unmanned systems, cryptography, critical infrastructure, or dual-use technology may create risks beyond the immediate research objective.

Such work should identify lawful purpose, authorized operators, access controls, data handling requirements, disclosure procedures, and foreseeable misuse.

Public release may require redaction, delayed publication, synthetic examples, restricted datasets, removal of exploit-enabling details, or coordination with affected parties.

The existence of public information does not automatically justify republishing it in a form that increases harm, exposure, or operational risk.

Research limitation

Projects published through this site are intended for lawful research, defensive engineering, education, authorized testing, public-interest analysis, and responsible technical development.

Research Status

How work is classified

Status Meaning Publication expectation
Active Current work receiving regular development, testing, collection, or analysis. Updates, code, notes, or releases may appear frequently.
Research Investigative or exploratory work without a final implementation or confirmed conclusion. Methods and provisional findings may be published with explicit uncertainty.
Development A system or prototype is being implemented and tested. Architecture, screenshots, test builds, and source may be published incrementally.
Experimental High uncertainty, early-stage work, or methods not yet validated. Results should not be treated as production claims.
Paused Work is temporarily inactive but remains potentially viable. Existing records remain available where appropriate.
Archived Work is no longer active but is retained for historical or technical value. Final state, limitations, and archival context should be preserved.
Restricted Work contains sensitive material, controlled data, contractual limits, or security risk. Only summaries, sanitized examples, or approved disclosures may be public.

Collaboration

Research collaboration

Collaboration may involve independent researchers, engineers, artists, institutions, laboratories, universities, companies, public agencies, nonprofit organizations, and technical communities.

Collaboration should begin with a clear problem, defined roles, data boundaries, ownership terms, publication expectations, security responsibilities, and a realistic understanding of available resources.

Sensitive work may require identity verification, written authorization, compartmentalized access, nondisclosure agreements, secure communication, and formal review.

Public-interest, open-source, educational, or reproducibility-focused proposals are particularly relevant where they align with the research mission.
