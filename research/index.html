Purpose

Research should create understanding, capability, and durable evidence.

Research is treated as a practical process of observation, collection, comparison, modeling, experimentation, implementation, verification, revision, and publication.

The objective is not merely to produce conclusions. It is to preserve enough evidence, source material, methodology, code, configuration, and context that another person can examine how those conclusions were reached.

Projects may produce software, hardware, firmware, datasets, maps, visualizations, technical papers, laboratory records, photographic documentation, prototypes, or operational systems.

Where public release is lawful and responsible, research should be published in a form that others can inspect, reproduce, challenge, extend, compare, or preserve.

Methods

Research methods and evidentiary practice.

Different disciplines require different methods, but all work should preserve provenance, assumptions, uncertainty, and enough context for later review.

Define the question

State the problem, scope, operational context, intended use, exclusions, assumptions, dependencies, and criteria by which the work will be evaluated.

Collect primary material

Preserve original documents, measurements, source code, logs, images, recordings, datasets, artifacts, interviews, and field notes wherever lawful and appropriate.

Record provenance

Track source, author, date, collection method, license, checksum, version, location, acquisition conditions, and chain of custody where relevant.

Separate fact from interpretation

Distinguish observed evidence, measured values, derived values, model output, assumptions, inference, opinion, recollection, hypothesis, and unresolved uncertainty.

Build reproducible workflows

Preserve scripts, configuration, versions, dependencies, hardware details, input data, processing steps, output checksums, and test procedures.

Test competing explanations

Avoid selecting only evidence that supports an initial conclusion. Compare alternative hypotheses and identify what evidence would support or disprove each one.

Publish limitations

State where data is incomplete, tools are experimental, samples are biased, measurements are uncertain, dependencies are unstable, or conclusions remain provisional.

Archive the process

Preserve not only the final report, but also methods, revisions, failed attempts, negative results, source material, design decisions, and deprecated approaches.

Workflow

From initial question to durable publication.

Stage 01

Question and Scope

Define the research question, operational context, intended audience, practical constraints, exclusions, risks, and success criteria.

Stage 02

Collection and Preservation

Gather primary sources, measurements, code, records, media, datasets, logs, artifacts, and field notes while preserving provenance and integrity.

Stage 03

Analysis and Modeling

Structure the material, test hypotheses, build models, compare alternatives, identify gaps, and document assumptions and uncertainty.

Stage 04

Implementation and Experiment

Build software, hardware, firmware, prototypes, simulations, diagrams, datasets, or controlled experiments needed to test the research.

Stage 05

Verification and Review

Repeat measurements, test edge cases, inspect failure modes, review code, compare outputs, validate evidence, and revise conclusions.

Stage 06

Publication and Archive

Release the results, methods, source, evidence, limitations, licenses, signatures, checksums, and archival record where lawful and responsible.

Standards

Minimum standards for published research.

Every published research project should identify

  • The research question or technical problem
  • Scope, exclusions, assumptions, and intended use
  • Methods, tools, and experimental conditions
  • Data sources and provenance
  • Dependencies and operating environment
  • Security, privacy, legal, and ethical considerations
  • Known limitations and unresolved uncertainty
  • Licensing, attribution, ownership, and disclosure terms

Every reproducible technical project should include

  • Source code or implementation files
  • Installation, build, and deployment instructions
  • Version, platform, and dependency information
  • Example input and expected output
  • Testing and validation procedures
  • Checksums, signatures, or release hashes where practical
  • Change history and revision status
  • Contact or responsible-disclosure channel

Data Governance

Research data must remain attributable, controlled, and appropriately protected.

Datasets should record source, collection time, jurisdiction, license, consent conditions, transformations, derived values, retention policy, integrity checks, access rules, and publication status.

Personal data, security-sensitive information, controlled records, confidential material, biological information, or contractually restricted data may require redaction, aggregation, encryption, restricted access, or nonpublication.

Public availability does not automatically make data ethically appropriate to aggregate, enrich, republish, operationalize, or expose in a more harmful form.

Where synthetic, anonymized, or minimized datasets can answer the research question, they should be preferred over unnecessary exposure of identifiable individuals or sensitive infrastructure.

Ethics and Responsibility

Research capability requires explicit boundaries.

Research involving cybersecurity, surveillance, investigations, sensitive personal data, unmanned systems, cryptography, critical infrastructure, biological systems, or dual-use technology may create risks beyond the immediate research objective.

Such work should identify lawful purpose, authorized operators, access controls, data-handling requirements, disclosure procedures, safety limits, and foreseeable misuse.

Public release may require redaction, delayed publication, synthetic examples, restricted datasets, removal of operationally dangerous detail, or coordination with affected parties.

The existence of public information does not automatically justify republishing it in a form that increases harm, exposure, targeting, or operational risk.

Publication

Research publication and release policy.

Public research should include sufficient context to distinguish finished results from drafts, working notes, experiments, speculative concepts, preliminary findings, and planned work.

Where possible, publications should include version numbers, dates, authorship, contributors, methods, source references, licensing, signatures, checksums, and revision history.

Security-sensitive, privacy-sensitive, legally restricted, contractually protected, unpublished, dual-use, or operationally dangerous material may be withheld, redacted, delayed, summarized, or released only to authorized parties.

Publication does not imply that every underlying dataset, artifact, system, source, or operational detail is publicly available.

Verification

Authenticity, signatures, hashes, and provenance.

OpenPGP signatures

Selected reports, releases, archives, and statements may be signed using published OpenPGP keys.

Cryptographic hashes

Files, datasets, software releases, images, archives, and disk images may include SHA-256, SHA3-256, or other published checksums.

Versioned releases

Research artifacts should use explicit version numbers, release dates, commit references, revision notes, and immutable archives where practical.

Source provenance

Research should identify the origin, acquisition method, transformation history, licensing, and limitations of source material.

Independent verification

Important identities, fingerprints, addresses, datasets, claims, and release artifacts should be confirmed through more than one trusted channel.

Archival preservation

Important releases may be mirrored, printed, signed, hashed, stored offline, or preserved across multiple systems and jurisdictions.
